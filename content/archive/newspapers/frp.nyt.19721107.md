<h3>Eviction of Indians Delayed on Appeal</h3>


<p>Source: New York Times, p. 73</p>

<p>Date: November 07, 1972</p>

<p>Author(s): William M. Blair</p>

<p>A Federal judge gave the Government permission today to retake the Bureau of Indian Affairs building from militant Indians, but a Federal appeals court stayed the order until Wednesday.</p>

<p>The stay prevented a possible clash between the police and the Indians, who were prepared to fight for the building that they have occupied since last Thursday.</p>

<p>The United States Court of Appeals issued its stay order a few hours after Judge H. Pratt of the United States District Court held the protesters in contempt of court and gave the Government authority to evict them.</p>

<p>The three-judge appeals court delayed enforcement of Judge Pratt's order until 9 P.M. Wednesday, and directed that negotiations between the Indians and Government officials continue.</p>

<p>Judge Pratt had said that the Indians had "flagrantly violated" a temporary restraining order he issued last week directing them to vacate the property. Counsel for the Indians carried their appeal to the higher court.</p>

<p>Federal officials were not unhappy with the appeals court order. While believing that the Indians would have to be ousted by force eventually, they felt that any action should be held up until after tomorrow's election.</p>

<p>At the Indian Affairs bureau, the protesters, numbering about 500, appeared to be making preparations against any attempt to dislodge them. Many of them were armed with improvised clubs and spears made of knives lashed to poles. Some had smeared their faces with lipstick in simulation of war paint.</p>

<p>'Any Weapons'</p>

<p>Russell Means, a leader of the protest, said that 200 Indians had taken a vow to fight to the death. The Indians will use, he said, "any weapons we can lay our hands on" but said that none of the group had any firearms.</p>

<p>Mr. Means, 32 years old, is a national field director of the American Indian Movement, which has led young activist Indians in protests against "broken treaties and promises" that they say have stalled Indian efforts to gain greater economic educational, health and other benefits.</p>

<p>They have also protested against the conflict within the Department of the Interior, of which the bureau is a part, over taking Indian lands for water and mineral resources and called for the bureau to be set up under separate control of Indians.</p>

<p>Secretary of the Interior Rogers C. B. Morton described the protesters tonight as a "splinter group of militants."</p>

<p>"It is obvious to me that the seizure and continued occupation of the building are nothing more than a form of blackmail by a small group which seek to achieve through violence objectives which are not supported by a majority of reservation Indians," he said in a statement.</p>

<p>The White House had overruled any effort to evict the Indians pending the outcome of negotiations with Federal officials and a final court decision. Some Interior officials had sought immediate action against the protesters when they seized the building Thursday night.</p>

<p>The Indians deliberately chose the eve of the Presidential election to dramatize their demands. They sought unsuccessfully to meet with President Nixon and his top aides.</p>

<p>They named their caravan to Washington "The Trail of Broken Treaties." The main body comes by bus, station wagons and car across the country from the West Coast, picking up adherents along the way. Anther caravan came from North Carolina.</p>

<p>This morning, as the Government went back to court to get an eviction order, Vernon Bellecourt, a director of A.I.M., told the militants, "We have now declared war on the United States of America--see your stations.</p>

<p>A few women and children remained in the building at 20th Street and Constitution Avenue, a few blocks from the White House, but most of them had been removed to housing arranged by volunteers. A tepee was set up on the building grounds facing Constitution Avenue, and Indian guards patrolled outside and inside the six-story structure.</p>

<p>No Time Set</p>

<p>Judge Pratt made his order effective at 6 P.M. but left it up to the Government when to enforce the order.</p>

<p>"The Government is going to retake that building and as soon as the Indians comply, the better off they will be," he said.</p>

<p>James Heller, an attorney for the Indians, sought more time for negotiations with Government officials who have met periodically for five days over the demands.</p>

<p>"Time is something we have," Mr. Heller said. "Blood is something we can't afford."</p>

<p>The choice, he said, was between minor property damage and "the dreadful possibility of bloodshed."</p>

<p>Judge Pratt issued his initial restraining order directing the Indians to leave the building last Friday but directed that Indian Affairs Bureau and Interior Department officials continue to negotiate.</p>

<p>The Indians demanded, in addition to immediate executive and Congressional actions on new treaties and land reform, housing and other accomodations during their stay. The Government countered with--and the Indians rejected--a Federal auditorium nearby and trailers for sanitary facilities but no food or financial assistance.</p>
